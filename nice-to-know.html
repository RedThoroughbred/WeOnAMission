‚ö†Ô∏è High Altitude Destination Ahuac is at 10,692 feet (3,260m) above sea level. This is considered high altitude. Most people experience some adjustment, but it's manageable with proper preparation.
What is Altitude Sickness?
‚ñ∂

Altitude sickness (acute mountain sickness - AMS) happens when your body doesn't get enough oxygen at high elevations. Symptoms typically appear 12-24 hours after arrival and can include:

  • Headache (most common)
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Nausea or loss of appetite
  • Shortness of breath
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Sleep disturbances

Good news: Most symptoms are mild and go away within 24-48 hours as your body acclimates.

Prevention & Treatment
‚ñ∂

Before the trip:

  • Get good sleep and rest
  • Stay hydrated (drink plenty of water)
  • Avoid alcohol and heavy meals
  • Consider talking to a doctor about altitude medication

During the first days:

  • Drink 3-4 liters of water per day
  • Eat light, frequent meals
  • Avoid alcohol for at least 48 hours
  • Take it easy physically
  • Try coca tea (local remedy - very popular!)
  • Sleep with head elevated

Currency

Peru uses the Sol (PEN). 1 USD ‚âà 3-4 Soles. ATMs are available in major towns. Bring some cash for remote areas.

Language

Spanish is the primary language. In tourist areas, some English is spoken. Learning a few phrases helps and locals appreciate the effort!

Weather (Dry Season)

June-July (our trip time) is the dry season. Days are cool (50-60¬∞F), nights are cold (32-40¬∞F). Rain is rare but possible.

Food

Peruvian cuisine is delicious and diverse. Expect rice, potatoes, corn, quinoa, and local meats. Vegetarian options available.

Water & Health

Stick to bottled water, especially in small towns. Most travelers' stomachs adjust fine. Bring anti-diarrheal medication just in case.

Safety

Peru is generally safe for tourists. We'll stay together as a group, avoid valuables display, and follow local guidance.

ü§ùCultural Tips

Showing Respect
‚ñ∂
  • Greetings: Always greet with "Buenos d√≠as" (morning), "Buenas tardes" (afternoon), or "Buenas noches" (evening)
  • Dress modestly: Especially at the school and in churches. Cover shoulders and knees
  • Ask before photographing: Always ask permission before taking photos of people
  • Eye contact: Direct eye contact shows respect and honesty
  • Handshakes: A firm handshake is customary for introductions
  • Personal space: Peruvians tend to stand closer during conversation than Americans
  • Don't point fingers: Use an open hand to gesture
  • Loud voices: Keep your voice moderate - loud talking can be seen as aggressive
Money & Tipping
‚ñ∂

Tipping practices in Peru:

  • Restaurants: 10% tip is customary if service was good (not always included)
  • Taxis: No tip needed (but rounding up is nice)
  • Guides: $5-10 USD per person for full-day guides
  • Hotels: $1-2 per night for housekeeping is appreciated

Bargaining: In markets, light haggling is acceptable and even expected, but be respectful and friendly.
